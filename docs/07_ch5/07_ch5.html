Learning Objectives

  • Explain the one-way ANOVA model for observational studies and randomized experiments.
  • State and interpret the three types of hypotheses: pairwise, omnibus, and one-group-vs-rest.
  • Compute and interpret the pooled variance estimate \(s_p^2\).
  • Conduct pairwise \(t\)-tests using the pooled variance.
  • Set up and interpret the ANOVA \(F\)-test using the extra-sum-of-squares framework.
  • Read a nested ANOVA table produced by R.
  • Check ANOVA assumptions with residual plots.
  • Describe the random-effects model and the intraclass correlation.

Case Study 5.1.2: The Spock Conspiracy Trial

In 1968, Dr. Benjamin Spock was tried in Boston on charges related to encouraging draft resistance during the Vietnam War. The defense argued that the Spock trial judge (referred to here as “Spock’s” judge) systematically underrepresented women on jury venires. They compared this judge’s venires with those of six other Boston-area district judges (labeled A–F).

This is an observational study: judge assignment to cases was not randomized.

The response is Percent — the percentage of women on each venire. We are interested in:

  1. Is Spock’s judge’s mean percent-women lower than the other judges?
  2. Are the other judges’ means all the same?

The Statistical Model

In each case study we are comparing means across \(I\) groups. We observe \(n_i\) individuals in group \(i\), for a total of \(n = n_1 + n_2 + \cdots + n_I\) observations.

Notation:

Symbol Meaning
\(\mu_i\) True population mean for group \(i\), \(i = 1, \ldots, I\)
\(Y_{ij}\) Observed value for the \(j\)th individual in group \(i\)
\(\varepsilon_{ij}\) Individual-specific noise for the \(j\)th individual in group \(i\): mean 0, variance \(\sigma^2\)

Model: \[Y_{ij} = \mu_i + \varepsilon_{ij}\]

In words: each observed value equals the true group mean plus a random individual-level noise term. The noise captures everything we can’t explain — genetic variation between mice, measurement error, day-to-day fluctuation in a judge’s venire composition, etc.

The key restriction is equal variance: \(\sigma^2\) is assumed the same for every group and every individual. A mouse in the N/R40 diet group has the same background noise variance as a mouse in the NP group; they just have different expected lifetimes. This is the \(I\)-group generalization of the pooled two-sample \(t\)-test from Chapter 2.

Spock trial interpretation:

  • \(\mu_1\) = true mean percent-women for Spock’s judge across all possible venires
  • \(\mu_i\) = true mean percent-women for judge \(i\), \(i = 2, \ldots, 7\) (judges A–F)
  • \(Y_{ij}\) = observed percent women on venire \(j\) of judge \(i\)
  • \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) = random variation in how that particular venire was drawn

Observational vs. randomized: The model equation \(Y_{ij} = \mu_i + \varepsilon_{ij}\) looks the same in both settings, but the randomized experiment has a useful additional interpretation. In the diet study, the same individual mouse could in principle have been assigned to any diet. If we call the response under diet 1 (N/N85, the control) \(Y^* = \mu_1 + \varepsilon_j\), then the same mouse under diet \(i\) would have response:

\[Y_j^{(i)} = \mu_i + \varepsilon_j = (\mu_i - \mu_1) + \mu_1 + \varepsilon_j = \delta_i + Y^*\]

where \(\delta_i = \mu_i - \mu_1\) is the treatment effect of diet \(i\) relative to the control. Because diets were randomly assigned, \(\delta_i\) has a genuine causal interpretation: it is how much longer (or shorter) a mouse would live if assigned to diet \(i\) instead of the control.

In an observational study we cannot make this argument — the “same individual under a different group” is a counterfactual we cannot observe, and confounding may explain any observed difference.

The difference is in the conclusions:

  • Observational study (Spock trial): use associational language (“is associated with”, “differs between”).
  • Randomized experiment (diet study): use causal language (“causes”, “results in”, “affects”).

Hypotheses

With \(I\) groups there are many possible hypotheses.

Pairwise Hypotheses

Test whether any specific pair of means differs: \[H_0: \mu_2 = \mu_5, \qquad H_A: \mu_2 \neq \mu_5\]

With \(I = 7\) groups there are \(\binom{7}{2} = 21\) possible pairwise tests.

Omnibus Hypothesis (“Are any means different?”)

\[H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \mu_3 = \mu_4 = \mu_5 = \mu_6 = \mu_7\] \[H_A: \text{at least one } \mu_i \neq \mu_j\]

One Group vs. the Rest

\[H_0: \mu_1 = \tfrac{1}{6}(\mu_2 + \mu_3 + \mu_4 + \mu_5 + \mu_6 + \mu_7)\] \[H_A: \mu_1 \neq \tfrac{1}{6}(\mu_2 + \mu_3 + \mu_4 + \mu_5 + \mu_6 + \mu_7)\]

This asks: Is Spock’s judge’s mean different from the average of the other judges?

Estimation

Group Sample Means

Estimate \(\mu_i\) with the group sample mean: \[\bar{Y}_{i\bullet} = \frac{1}{n_i}\sum_{j=1}^{n_i} Y_{ij}\]

Pooled Variance

Each group has its own sample standard deviation \(s_i\), measuring the spread of observations within that group. Because we assume the same true variance \(\sigma^2\) for every group, we can combine these within-group estimates into one more accurate estimate. We weight each group by the amount of information it contributes — groups with more observations get more weight:

\[s_p^2 = \frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2 + (n_2-1)s_2^2 + \cdots + (n_I-1)s_I^2} {(n_1-1) + (n_2-1) + \cdots + (n_I-1)}\]

In words: each term \((n_i-1)s_i^2\) in the numerator is the sum of squared deviations from group \(i\)’s mean. Adding these across all groups gives the total within-group variation. Dividing by the total degrees of freedom gives the average within-group variance.

The denominator is \(\nu = n - I\) (the total degrees of freedom available for estimating variance within groups — we lose one degree of freedom per group because we estimated each group’s mean).

Two observations: - This is a weighted average of the within-group variance estimates, weighted by their degrees of freedom. - When \(I = 2\) this reduces exactly to the pooled variance from Chapter 2.

Testing Pairwise Differences

To test \(H_0: \mu_i = \mu_j\) vs \(H_A: \mu_i \neq \mu_j\), we build a \(t\)-statistic in three steps.

Step 1 — Distribution of the difference under \(H_0\). Each group mean is approximately normal: \(\bar{Y}_{i\bullet} \sim N(\mu_i,\; \sigma^2/n_i)\). Under \(H_0\) (\(\mu_i = \mu_j\)), the two means are independent, so their difference is: \[\bar{Y}_{i\bullet} - \bar{Y}_{j\bullet} \;\sim\; N\!\left(0,\; \sigma^2\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n_i} + \tfrac{1}{n_j}\right)\right)\]

Step 2 — Standardize. Dividing by the true standard deviation gives a standard normal: \[\frac{\bar{Y}_{i\bullet} - \bar{Y}_{j\bullet}} {\sigma\sqrt{1/n_i + 1/n_j}} \;\sim\; N(0,1)\]

Step 3 — Estimate \(\sigma\). We don’t know \(\sigma\), so we substitute the pooled estimate \(s_p\). Replacing a known standard deviation with an estimate inflates uncertainty, and the result follows a \(t\) distribution:

\[t^* = \frac{\bar{Y}_{i\bullet} - \bar{Y}_{j\bullet}}{s_p\sqrt{1/n_i + 1/n_j}} \;\sim\; t_\nu \quad \text{under } H_0\]

where \(\nu = n - I\).

Why \(\nu = n - I\)? Because \(s_p^2\) uses all \(n\) observations across all \(I\) groups to estimate \(\sigma^2\) — not just the \(n_i + n_j\) observations in the two groups being compared. Using more data gives a better estimate, which in turn gives larger degrees of freedom (\(n - I\) instead of \(n_i + n_j - 2\)), making the \(t\) distribution closer to a standard normal and the test more powerful.

The advantage over running separate two-sample \(t\)-tests is that we use all the data to estimate \(\sigma^2\), giving more degrees of freedom and a more accurate variance estimate.

A 95% confidence interval for \(\mu_i - \mu_j\) is:

\[\bar{Y}_{i\bullet} - \bar{Y}_{j\bullet} \;\pm\; t_{\nu}(0.975) \cdot s_p\sqrt{1/n_i + 1/n_j}\]

WarningMultiple Comparisons

Running 21 pairwise tests at \(\alpha = 0.05\) inflates the overall Type I error rate well above 5%. Chapter 6 covers methods that control for multiple comparisons. For now, we treat each pairwise test independently and note the concern.

The ANOVA \(F\)-Test

For the omnibus hypothesis (\(H_0\): all means equal vs \(H_A\): at least two differ), a single summary statistic is needed. The ANOVA \(F\)-test compares two nested models by asking: how much does model fit improve when we allow separate group means?

Full and Reduced Models

Reduced model (\(H_0\)) Full model (\(H_A\))
Means \(\mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_I = \mu\) (one shared mean) \(\mu_1, \mu_2, \ldots, \mu_I\) (separate means)
Estimates \(\hat{\mu} = \bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\hat{\mu}_i = \bar{Y}_{i\bullet}\)
# parameters 1 \(I\)

For the Spock data (\(I = 7\) judges), here is what each model predicts for each group:

Group Judge Full model prediction Reduced model prediction
1 Spock’s \(\bar{Y}_{1\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)
2 Judge A \(\bar{Y}_{2\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)
3 Judge B \(\bar{Y}_{3\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)
4 Judge C \(\bar{Y}_{4\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)
5 Judge D \(\bar{Y}_{5\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)
6 Judge E \(\bar{Y}_{6\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)
7 Judge F \(\bar{Y}_{7\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\)

In the full model, each group gets its own sample mean as a prediction. Spock’s judge’s venires are predicted by Spock’s judge’s average; Judge A’s venires are predicted by Judge A’s average; and so on. There are 7 free parameters — one per judge.

In the reduced model, every observation — regardless of judge — is predicted by the same grand average \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) (the mean over all 46 venires). This is the model that says \(H_0\) is true: if all judges have the same expected percent-women, then the best single prediction for any venire is just the overall average.

What is a residual? A residual is simply the distance between an observed value and the model’s prediction for it:

  • Full model residual: \(Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}_{i\bullet}\) — how far observation \((i,j)\) is from its group’s mean
  • Reduced model residual: \(Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) — how far observation \((i,j)\) is from the grand mean

Intuitively, if the groups really have different means, the full model residuals will be small (each observation is close to its own group mean), while the reduced model residuals will be large (observations from Spock’s group, for example, are far from the overall grand mean).

Sums of Squared Residuals

The residual sum of squares (RSS) for each model is:

\[RSS_{full} = \sum_i \sum_j (Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}_{i\bullet})^2, \quad df_{full} = n - I\]

\[RSS_{reduced} = \sum_i \sum_j (Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet})^2, \quad df_{reduced} = n - 1\]

Each RSS adds up all the squared residuals across every observation. Think of it as a scoreboard for how badly the model is fitting: a small RSS means predictions are close to the data; a large RSS means they are far away. Both RSS values involve squaring (so positive and negative residuals don’t cancel) and summing (so we get a single overall measure).

The extra sum of squares (ESS) measures how much the fit improves when we go from the reduced model to the full model:

\[ESS = RSS_{reduced} - RSS_{full}, \quad df_{extra} = I - 1\]

Why is \(RSS_{reduced} \geq RSS_{full}\) always? The full model has the reduced model as a special case: if all the \(\mu_i\) happen to be equal, the full model’s separate group means would all estimate the same value as the grand mean, and the two models would give identical fits. In general, the full model has more flexibility, so it can never fit worse than the reduced model — it can always do at least as well. Therefore \(ESS \geq 0\) always.

What does ESS tell us? If \(H_0\) is true (all means really are equal), fitting separate group means should not improve the fit by very much — the data don’t contain strong evidence for distinct groups. In that case, ESS will be positive but small. If the means truly differ (the data come from groups with different means), the full model will fit much better, and ESS will be large. The \(F\)-test formalizes what “small” and “large” mean.

The plots below illustrate this for the Spock data. Red bars/lines show the model’s fitted means; larger gaps between points and those lines = larger residuals.

The squared residuals are much smaller under the full model, confirming that group means differ substantially.

The \(F\)-Statistic

If \(H_0\) is true, both \(ESS/df_{extra}\) and \(RSS_{full}/df_{full} = s_p^2\) estimate the same \(\sigma^2\), so their ratio should be near 1. If \(H_A\) is true, the means differ and \(ESS\) will be inflated, pushing the ratio above 1.

\[F = \frac{ESS / df_{extra}}{s_p^2} = \frac{ESS / (I-1)}{RSS_{full} / (n-I)}\]

Under \(H_0\), \(F \sim F_{I-1,\; n-I}\). The \(p\)-value uses only the upper tail (large \(F\) is evidence against \(H_0\)).

The \(F\)-Distribution

The \(F\) distribution is parameterized by two degrees of freedom: numerator (\(df_1\)) and denominator (\(df_2\)). Below are a few shapes:

\(p\)-Value for the Spock Omnibus Test

The Spock data has \(I = 7\) judges and \(n = 46\) venires, so \(df_{extra} = 6\) and \(df_{full} = 39\).

A Hierarchy of Models

In more complex questions we compare three or more nested models. Each model is a special case of the next:

  • Null: all means equal \((\mu)\)
  • Intermediate: Spock’s judge differs from a common mean for the rest \((\mu_\text{Spock}, \mu_\text{others})\)
  • Full: all seven means free \((\mu_1, \mu_2, \ldots, \mu_7)\)

Here is what each model predicts for each judge in the Spock data.

Null vs. Intermediate — Is Spock’s judge different from the rest?

Group Judge Null (reduced) Intermediate (full)
1 Spock’s \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{1\bullet}\)
2 Judge A \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{0}\)
3 Judge B \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{0}\)
4 Judge C \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{0}\)
5 Judge D \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{0}\)
6 Judge E \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{0}\)
7 Judge F \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{0}\)

Here \(\bar{Y}_{\bullet\bullet}\) is the grand mean of all 46 venires, and \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) is the average percent-women across all venires belonging to judges A–F (groups 2 through 7). The intermediate model has 2 parameters: one for Spock’s judge (\(\bar{Y}_{1\bullet}\)) and one pooled mean for the remaining six judges (\(\bar{Y}_{0}\)).

Intermediate vs. Full — Do judges A–F differ among themselves?

Group Judge Intermediate (reduced) Full
1 Spock’s \(\bar{Y}_{1\bullet}\) \(\bar{Y}_{1\bullet}\)
2 Judge A \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) \(\bar{Y}_{2\bullet}\)
3 Judge B \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) \(\bar{Y}_{3\bullet}\)
4 Judge C \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) \(\bar{Y}_{4\bullet}\)
5 Judge D \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) \(\bar{Y}_{5\bullet}\)
6 Judge E \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) \(\bar{Y}_{6\bullet}\)
7 Judge F \(\bar{Y}_{0}\) \(\bar{Y}_{7\bullet}\)

This test asks: after accounting for Spock’s judge, do the remaining six judges’ venires show any systematic differences from each other?

Notice that we always fit both models using all the data — we never discard Spock’s judge’s observations even when comparing only judges A–F. This matters for the variance estimate: instead of testing on a subset of 39 venires (judges A–F only, \(df = 32\)), we keep all 46 venires and use \(s_p^2\) from the full model (\(df = 39\)). More degrees of freedom means a better variance estimate and a more powerful test.

Each \(F\)-test compares adjacent models. Crucially, all F-tests use the variance estimate \(s_p^2 = RSS_{full}/df_{full}\) from the most general (full) model, regardless of which pair is being compared.

The full sequential ANOVA table for the Spock data is:

Model \(df_{RSS}\) RSS \(df_{extra}\) ESS \(F\) \(p\)-value
All same 45 3792
Spock’s differs 44 2191 1 1601 33.48 <0.001
All different 39 1864 5 326 1.37 0.26

Conclusions:

  • Strong evidence that Spock’s judge has a different (lower) mean than the other judges (\(F_{1,39} = 33.48\), \(p < 0.001\)).
  • No evidence that the six non-Spock judges differ among themselves (\(F_{5,39} = 1.37\), \(p = 0.26\)).

Note: the \(F\)-statistic in the table uses \(s_p^2 = 1864/39 \approx 47.8\) (from the full model) as the denominator throughout.

Note\(s_p^2\) is not the same as the overall sample variance

The pooled variance \(s_p^2 = RSS_{full}/39 = 1864/39 \approx 48\) measures how much observations vary within groups — after accounting for differences in group means. It is what ANOVA uses to estimate \(\sigma^2\).

If you ignored the groups entirely and computed a single sample variance for all 46 venires, you would get \(s^2 = RSS_{reduced}/45 = 3792/45 \approx 84\). This is larger because it lumps together within-group and between-group variation — the judges have different means, and that extra spread inflates \(s^2\). Using \(s_p^2\) removes the between-group contribution and focuses on the within-group noise, giving a better estimate of \(\sigma^2\).

Assumptions and Diagnostics

The same three assumptions from the two-sample \(t\)-test apply, in the same order of importance:

  1. Independence of observations.
  2. Equal variance across all groups.
  3. Normality (approximately symmetric, no severe outliers).

New tool for checking assumptions 2 and 3: residual plots.

The residual for observation \((i,j)\) under the full model is: \[e_{ij} = Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}_{i\bullet}\]

Plotting residuals removes the group-mean structure, letting us focus on the variation about the mean without visual clutter from the differences in means between groups.

Residual Plots in R

The quickest way is plot() applied to an aov() object:

par(mfrow = c(2, 2))
plot(aout_full)
par(mfrow = c(1, 1))

The two most important panels:

  • Residuals vs. Fitted: residuals (\(y\)-axis) vs. group means (\(x\)-axis). Look for unequal spread across the fitted values.
  • Normal Q-Q: QQ-plot of residuals. Look for departures from a straight line (skew, heavy tails, outliers).

Random Effects

In the fixed-effects model above, the groups (judges, diets) are the specific groups of interest — we want to compare these particular groups.

Sometimes the groups are not of direct interest. Instead, they are a random sample from a larger population of groups, and we want to make inferences about variability among groups in that population.

Example: Quality control — measuring output of 7 randomly selected machines from a factory. We don’t care about those specific 7 machines; we want to know how much machine-to-machine variability exists across the entire factory floor.

When groups are a random sample from a larger population of groups, use the random-effects model.

The Random-Effects Model

The model is written in two layers:

\[Y_{ij} = \mu_i + \varepsilon_{ij}, \quad \text{var}(\varepsilon_{ij}) = \sigma^2\] \[\mu_i = \mu + \xi_i, \quad \text{var}(\xi_i) = \sigma_p^2\]

The first line is identical to the fixed-effects model. The second line says that each group mean \(\mu_i\) is itself random — it is the overall average \(\mu\) plus a random group-specific deviation \(\xi_i\). These deviations have mean 0 and variance \(\sigma_p^2\).

In words: every observation has two sources of randomness:

  1. Within-group noise \(\varepsilon_{ij}\): how much individual \(j\) in group \(i\) differs from its group’s mean (variance \(\sigma^2\)).
  2. Between-group noise \(\xi_i\): how much group \(i\)’s mean differs from the overall mean \(\mu\) (variance \(\sigma_p^2\)).

If \(\sigma_p^2 = 0\), all groups have the same true mean (\(\mu_i = \mu\) for all \(i\)), and there is no between-group variability at all.

Hypotheses: \[H_0: \sigma_p^2 = 0 \quad \text{(no between-group variability — all group means equal } \mu\text{)}\] \[H_A: \sigma_p^2 > 0 \quad \text{(some groups have higher/lower means than others)}\]

We run the same \(F\)-test as before. Under \(H_0\), the \(F\)-statistic has the same \(F_{I-1,\, n-I}\) null distribution.

Why Use This Model?

In one-way ANOVA, the \(p\)-value from the \(F\)-test is identical whether you treat groups as fixed or random. So why bother with random effects?

In more complex models — for example, when you have multiple random factors, or when the same individuals appear in multiple groups — treating groups as random leads to different (and generally better) estimates and inference. The one-way case is the simplest introduction to this framework.

Intraclass Correlation (ICC)

The ICC measures the proportion of total variance explained by group membership:

\[\text{ICC} = \frac{\hat\sigma_p^2}{\hat\sigma_p^2 + \hat\sigma^2}\]

To see why: the total variance of any observation \(Y_{ij}\) is \(\sigma_p^2 + \sigma^2\) (between-group plus within-group). The ICC is the fraction of that total coming from between-group differences.

  • ICC close to 1: most variation is between groups — knowing the group an observation belongs to tells you most of what you need to know about its value.
  • ICC close to 0: most variation is within groups — group membership explains very little; individual-level noise dominates.
