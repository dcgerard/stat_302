Twin Study — Paired \(t\)-Test (Case Study 2.2.2)

Researchers measured left-hippocampus volumes (in \(\text{cm}^3\)) for 15 pairs of identical twins, one of whom had schizophrenia (Affected) and one of whom did not (Unaffected).

library(tidyverse)
case0202 <- read_csv("https://dcgerard.github.io/stat_302/data/case0202.csv")
glimpse(case0202)
Rows: 15
Columns: 2
$ Unaffected <dbl> 1.94, 1.44, 1.56, 1.58, 2.06, 1.66, 1.75, 1.77, 1.78, 1.92,…
$ Affected   <dbl> 1.27, 1.63, 1.47, 1.39, 1.93, 1.26, 1.71, 1.67, 1.28, 1.85,…

1. Each row in case0202 is a pair of twins rather than two independent individuals. Why is a paired \(t\)-test more appropriate here than a two-sample \(t\)-test?

2. Create a new vector diff containing the difference in left-hippocampus volume for each pair (Unaffected minus Affected).

3. Use t.test() to run a one-sample \(t\)-test on diff against \(H_0: \mu = 0\). Save the result as tout and print it.

4. Now carry out the test manually. Compute the mean, standard deviation, and sample size of diff.

5. Compute the \(t\)-statistic: \[t^* = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}\] Save it as tstat.

6. What are the degrees of freedom for this test? Save it as degfr.

7. Use pt() to compute the two-sided \(p\)-value from tstat and degfr. Check that it matches the output of t.test() from Question 3.

8. Manually construct the 95% confidence interval for \(\mu\): \[\bar{d} \;\pm\; t_{n-1}(0.975) \cdot \frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}\] Use qt(0.975, df = degfr) for the critical value.

9. Interpret the 95% confidence interval from Question 8 in the context of this study. What does it say about the magnitude of the hippocampus volume difference?

10. Based on Questions 3–9, write a complete statistical conclusion. Address: (a) whether there is evidence of a difference, (b) the estimated magnitude and direction of the difference, and (c) a 95% CI.

11. The Central Limit Theorem underpins the \(t\)-test. State the CLT in your own words and explain why it allows us to use a \(t\)-distribution here even if hippocampus volumes are not perfectly normally distributed in the population.

Beak Study — Two-Sample \(t\)-Test (Case Study 2.1.1)

Researchers measured beak depths (mm) of Galápagos finches on Daphne Major in 1976 (before a drought) and in 1978 (after a drought that favored larger, harder seeds).

case0201 <- read.csv("https://dcgerard.github.io/stat_302/data/case0201.csv") |>
  mutate(Year = factor(Year))
glimpse(case0201)
Rows: 178
Columns: 2
$ Year  <fct> 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976, 1976…
$ Depth <dbl> 6.2, 6.8, 7.1, 7.1, 7.4, 7.8, 7.9, 8.0, 8.2, 8.4, 8.4, 8.4, 8.5,…

1. Let \(\mu_1\) = population mean beak depth in 1976 and \(\mu_2\) = population mean beak depth in 1978. State the null and alternative hypotheses in words and in mathematical notation.

2. Make a boxplot of beak Depth (y-axis) by Year (x-axis). Based on the plot, do the two distributions appear to have similar spreads (variances)? What does this suggest about which version of the \(t\)-test to use?

3. Run a two-sample \(t\)-test assuming equal variances. Save the result as tout and print it.

t.test(formula = Depth ~ Year, data = case0201, var.equal = TRUE)

4. Write a complete statistical conclusion based on the output above. Include the estimated difference in means, the 95% CI, and the \(p\)-value.

5. The confidence interval from Question 3 gives a range of plausible values for \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\) (1976 minus 1978). Is the interval entirely above zero, entirely below zero, or does it straddle zero? What does that tell you?

6. Suppose the researchers had a directional prediction: beaks will be deeper in 1978 than in 1976 (i.e., the drought selected for larger beaks).

  1. State the one-sided alternative hypothesis in symbols.

  2. Add the appropriate argument to t.test() to run this one-sided test and report the resulting \(p\)-value. Is it larger or smaller than the two-sided \(p\)-value from Question 3? Explain why.

7. By default, t.test() uses var.equal = FALSE, which applies the Satterthwaite approximation to compute the degrees of freedom. In Question 3 we overrode this with var.equal = TRUE. Under what conditions is it safer to use var.equal = FALSE? Run the default version below and compare the degrees of freedom and \(p\)-value to Question 3.

8. In this study, finches were not randomly assigned to years — the year was determined by when the study took place. Does this affect whether we can claim that the drought caused a change in beak depth? What additional reasoning or evidence would be needed to support a causal claim?
