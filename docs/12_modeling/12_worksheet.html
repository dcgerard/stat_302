Pollution and Mortality

Does air pollution kill people? Data from an early study were collected for 60 Standard Metropolitan Statistical Areas (cities) in the United States, covering the years 1959–1961. The goal is to determine whether the pollution variables are associated with mortality after accounting for climate and socioeconomic differences between cities.

Variables:

Variable Description
CITY City name
Mortality Total age-adjusted mortality (deaths per 100,000)
Precip Mean annual precipitation (inches)
Humidity Percent relative humidity (annual average at 1 pm)
JanTemp Mean January temperature (°F)
JulyTemp Mean July temperature (°F)
Over65 Percent of population aged 65 or older
House Population per household
Educ Median school years completed (age 25+)
Sound Percent of housing that is sound with all facilities
Density Population density (persons per square mile)
NonWhite Percent of population that is non-white
WhiteCol Percent employed in white-collar occupations
Poor Percent of households with income under $3,000 (1960)
HC Relative pollution potential of hydrocarbons
NOX Relative pollution potential of nitrogen oxides
SO2 Relative pollution potential of sulfur dioxide

1. What are the observational units? What is the response variable? Which variables are the primary explanatory variables of interest (the pollution variables)? Which are considered control variables?

2. Make scatterplots of each pollution variable (HC, NOX, SO2) against Mortality. Based on these plots, do any transformations seem appropriate?

3. Create log-transformed versions of the three pollution variables. Then make new scatterplots using the log-transformed versions. Does the log transformation help?

4. Fit a rich model that includes all non-pollution control variables (no interactions, no transformations of the controls needed):

lm_rich <- lm(Mortality ~ Precip + Humidity + JanTemp + JulyTemp +
                Over65 + House + Educ + Sound + Density +
                NonWhite + WhiteCol + Poor,
              data = ex1217)

Create a residual plot and a QQ-plot of the residuals. Do the model assumptions look reasonable?

5. Compute leverage values, studentized residuals, and Cook’s distances for the rich model from Question 4. Produce plots of each against observation number. Are there any cities you are particularly concerned about?

6. Use step() on the rich model from Question 4 to select a subset of control variables. Do not include the pollution variables in this step — we will add them separately. What variables were retained?

7. Write out the full regression model you will use to test for a pollution effect. The model should include the variables selected in Question 6 plus the three log-transformed pollution variables.

8. Fit the model from Question 7. Use anova() with the stepwise-selected model (no pollution variables) as the reduced model and the model with pollution as the full model.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_A\) for this \(F\)-test.

  2. Report the \(F\)-statistic, degrees of freedom, and \(p\)-value.

  3. State a conclusion in the context of the study.

9. Use tidy(lm_final, conf.int = TRUE) to examine the individual pollution coefficients. Are any of the pollution variables individually significant? Do the signs of the coefficients make intuitive sense? If not, what might explain a counterintuitive sign?

10. Rerun the stepwise selection and \(F\)-test from Questions 6 and 8, but this time exclude the high-leverage cities you identified in Question 5. Does the conclusion about the pollution-mortality association change? What does this tell you about the robustness of the result?
